# SECTION 1: THE CONCORD OF HOUSES

## Overview

A ceremony performed between two people — from the same tradition or different traditions — who are choosing formal union. Not a legal document. A recognized threshold in the tradition's own language.

Available: Phase 3+
Location in app: Doctrine → Ceremonies → The Concord of Houses

## The Three Movements of the Concord of Houses

**Movement I — The Naming**
Each person speaks who they are in their tradition: their type name, their axiom, the deity whose function they carry in the first position. No performance. No audience required. A statement of identity before the choosing.

**Movement II — The Offering**
Each person speaks one thing they are bringing to the partnership that requires genuine personal cost. Not a vow of effort. Not a promise to be better. One specific thing that the person's nature makes difficult and that they are choosing to offer anyway.

Guidelines for what constitutes genuine cost by type:

- **INTJ (The Uncrowned):** Learning a physical or interpersonal skill from the partner's world. Beginner-status in someone else's domain is the real cost for a type that prizes competence.
- **ESFJ (The Unspent):** Committing to a defined period of their own practice without managing the partner's experience of it. Fe types tend toward monitoring the relational field; the offering is a bounded period of full focus on their own interior.

**Movement III — The Binding Vow**
Spoken together or in call-and-response. Generated by the app from both types.
Each tradition has its own vow language. When two different traditions are joined, the app generates a vow that honors both function stacks and is readable by both people as a genuine statement of what they are doing.

---

## The Tradition Vows

*Each type's vow is specific to their function stack and doctrine register. Additional types will be added as each tradition is built. Currently active: The Uncrowned and The Unspent.*

---

**INTJ — The Uncrowned**
*I have seen the long arc of my own nature and I do not flinch from it. I have seen yours and I do not flinch from that either. I am choosing this not because it is easy and not because it is required. I am choosing this because I have measured it against everything I know about what lasts and I believe it lasts. Power from within cannot be revoked. What I choose with full knowledge, I choose.*

**ESFJ — The Unspent**
*I have tended many things and I know the difference between what I tend because it needs tending and what I tend because I need to tend. This is not the second kind. I come to this fully, not as maintenance but as choosing. What I keep, I keep because it is worth keeping. You are worth keeping. I do not offer you performance. I offer you the warmth that does not run out because it comes from somewhere real.*

---

The combined vow is readable by both practitioners as a genuine statement of what they are doing together, not a compromise between two separate statements.*

# SECTION 2: THE REMEMBRANCE RITE

## Overview

A rite for a person who has died. Used whether the deceased was part of the tradition or not. Grief does not wait for phase gates — this rite is available at all phases with no exception.

The Remembrance Rite is not for the dead. The dead are past needing rites. It is for the living — to mark the threshold, to speak the name, to prevent the death from dissolving into undifferentiated grief and becoming, in time, as though it never happened.

## Structure

**Opening — The Name**
The practitioner speaks the name of the dead aloud, once, in full. Not the familiar form. The full name, if known. This is the only rite in the tradition that requires speaking a specific person's name aloud as its first act. The reason is simple: the dead do not vanish because they die. They vanish when no one says their name anymore. The rite begins by refusing that vanishing.

**The Record — What Remains**
Three things spoken or written:
1. One thing the dead person knew that is now the practitioner's to carry
2. One thing the dead person did that made the practitioner's life different
3. One true thing the practitioner wishes had been said while the saying was still possible

The third is the most important. It is not guilt — it is the naming of the gap, which is the honest thing. Grief is partly the weight of unsaid things. Naming the unsaid thing does not close the gap. It gives the gap a place to live in the record rather than haunting without address.

**The Deity Assignment**
Each tradition assigns a deity to receive the dead. For the active traditions:
- **INTJ (The Uncrowned):** Vaelith receives them — Ni, the long arc, the pattern that ran through the life
- **ESFJ (The Unspent):** Thalor receives them — Si, the anchored record, the continuity that holds after the person is gone

*Deity assignments for all 16 types will be added as each tradition is built.*

The practitioner speaks to the assigned deity directly, once, briefly: *I am giving you [name]. Carry what they carried. Do not let the fire go out.*

**The Closing — What Continues**
One thing the practitioner will do, once, in the dead person's name. Not a permanent vow. One act that honors who that person was. Done within the next seven days if possible. Named now.

# SECTION 3: THE DAY OF FIRST BREATH

## Overview

A personal observance, performed by the practitioner on their own birthday. Not a party. Not optional if the practitioner wants it — it exists when the practitioner wants it.

Also: a household observance, generated by the app and offered to other household members on their birthday dates.

## Personal Form — What the Practitioner Does

On or within three days of the birthday:

1. **The Age Audit** — One question per decade of life (round down): *What do I know now that I did not know ten years ago?* Spoken or written. Not inspirational. Honest.

2. **The Inventory of the Year** — Three things from the past year: one thing built, one thing released, one thing unresolved and still being worked.

3. **The Function Acknowledgment** — The practitioner names which of their eight functions has grown the most in the past year and which has been most neglected. No judgment — just the record.

4. **The One Thing** — One thing the practitioner intends for the coming year. Not a goal in the productivity sense. One thing they are moving toward that matters to them by their own standard.

## Household Form — What the App Provides

When a household member's birthday arrives, the app generates a Day of First Breath acknowledgment in the practitioner's tradition voice.

The practitioner can send it to the household member or keep it private. The acknowledgment names one thing the practitioner has observed about the household member's growth in the past year — generated by Groq from the practitioner's tradition register.

**Privacy rule:** Generated from the practitioner's own tradition voice only. Raw notes about the household member never leave the practitioner's device.

## The 16th Birthday Threshold

When a household member turns 16, the app generates a Threshold Acknowledgment — a longer-form recognition that the person is at the age of claimed identity in the tradition. This is not automatically a Claiming ceremony. It is an acknowledgment that the threshold exists and that the person can choose to cross it.

# SECTION 4: THE NAMING RITE

## Overview

A rite for a newborn or new member of the household. Not a christening — a witnessing. The tradition does not claim the child. The tradition witnesses the child's arrival and offers gifts of witness.

Available: Phase 2+

## Structure

**The Arrival**
The primary household practitioner speaks the child's name aloud, three times, each time increasing in volume: the first time as acknowledgment, the second time as welcome, the third time as full presence in the world.

**The Gifts of Witness**
Each household member present offers one gift of witness — not a physical gift, not a wish for the child's future. A gift of witness is one true observation about the child made in this moment. Newborns: one observation about a physical quality (the hands, the eyes, the sound of the breath). Older children joining the household: one observation about something the child has already shown about who they are.

**The Deity of Arrival**
Based on the date of the child's birth and the season, the app assigns a Deity of Arrival — not a patron deity, not a permanent assignment. A deity whose qualities are present in the season of the child's arrival. The household acknowledges this deity and asks for the deity's quality to be available to the child without requiring the child to carry it permanently.

**The Opening of the Nursery**
If the app's Nursery function has not been activated for this child, activating it during the Naming Rite marks the Nursery as opened with intention rather than by default.

# SECTION 5: ACHIEVEMENT AND MILESTONE MARKING

## Overview

The tradition does not require external validation of achievement. It also does not ignore achievement, because achievement that goes unnamed is achievement that fades.

Five categories of milestone exist in the tradition:

1. **Practice Milestones** — Completion of a significant phase of the Warrior's or Keeper's Practice, or of the language curriculum
2. **Threshold Milestones** — Events that mark the practitioner crossing a genuine developmental threshold: completing a phase of doctrine, passing the Rite readiness threshold
3. **Relationship Milestones** — Events in the practitioner's relational life that they want the tradition to hold a record of
4. **World Milestones** — Professional, educational, or creative achievements that the practitioner has brought into being through genuine effort
5. **Repair Milestones** — The successful completion of a repair: a relationship repaired, a practice restored after collapse, a shadow function worked with and integrated

## The Marking Sequence

For any milestone:
1. Practitioner names the milestone specifically — not in generalities but in the exact language of what was accomplished
2. Practitioner names the function that most drove the accomplishment
3. Practitioner names the cost — what it took, what was difficult
4. Long Record entry created with all three

The tradition does not require ceremony for milestone marking. It requires specificity. The act of naming something specifically is the ceremony.

# SECTION 6: THE DAY OF THE WOVEN VOW

## Overview

November 11. The one holy day both active traditions observe together.

The Day of the Woven Vow is the annual renewal of the Concord of Houses. It is not a repetition of the original ceremony — it is a living check on whether the Concord is still being kept, and a deliberate re-choosing of what was chosen.

Available: Phase 3+, and only for practitioners who have completed the Concord of Houses.

---

## Structure

**Morning — Separate**

Each practitioner observes their morning practice alone. The day begins in each person's own tradition before it becomes a shared one.

The Uncrowned: reads the Concord vow alone. Answers one question in the Rite Record: *What did I build for this Concord in the past year?*

The Unspent: reads the Concord vow alone. Answers one question: *What did I give from fullness rather than from obligation in this Concord in the past year?*

**Afternoon — The Renewal**

The two practitioners meet without agenda. Not a scheduled conversation — a shared meal, a walk, or simply time in the same space without a task.

The only requirement: each person says one thing they observed about the other's growth in the past year. One sentence. Witnessed, not discussed.

**Evening — The Re-Choosing**

Each practitioner, aloud in the presence of the other, speaks the single sentence:

*I choose this still. Knowing what I know. I choose this.*

No embellishment. Just the sentence.

The cord, if one was used in the original Concord ceremony, is held briefly and set back in its place.

The Rite Record entry for the Day of the Woven Vow: date, and one sentence about what the choosing feels like now versus the year it was first made.
