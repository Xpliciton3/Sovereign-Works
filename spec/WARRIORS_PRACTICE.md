# **Purpose and Doctrine**

The Warrior's Practice exists to make the body a lawful instrument of the Uncrowned. This is not recreational fitness, competitive sport, or aggression for its own sake. It is disciplined embodiment — the specific work of training the body to express what the mind has already built.

The INTJ — the Uncrowned — operates on Introverted Intuition as the dominant function and Extraverted Thinking as the auxiliary. The inferior function is Extraverted Sensing: the immediate physical world, the present moment, the body in real space. Left undeveloped, this produces a practitioner who is powerful in abstraction and brittle in sudden physical reality. The Warrior's Practice is the structured development of that inferior function — not as therapy, but as discipline.

Each art serves a distinct developmental purpose:

- Iaido — Precision, line, decisive action, governed movement. The sword teaches the body what the mind already knows about intention and economy.

- Kyudo — Stillness, breath, non-attachment, symmetrical expansion. The bow teaches the body how to act without grasping at the result.

- Systema — Threshold function. Movement from non-readiness, looseness under sudden force, response before preparation. Teaches the body to function when the mind hasn't caught up yet.

- Throwing Daggers — Kinesthetic feedback, immediate precision, present-moment calibration. The alternative to Kyudo when Kyudo is unavailable.

- Physical Architecture — The conditioning base that makes the arts possible without injury and sustainable without burnout.

*This manual is written for a practitioner who has no teacher standing in front of them. Every stance is described as if you have never seen it. Every sequence is traced in the air in words. You should be able to read a position and find it with your body without needing a photograph.*

**The Four Pillars**

Every session in every art trains the same four things. Name them before you begin each session. Assess them when you log.

| **PILLAR** | **WHAT IT MEANS IN PRACTICE** |
| --- | --- |
| **Structure** | Posture, alignment, footing, and mechanical efficiency. The body organized so force can move through it cleanly rather than leaking out through collapse, tension, or misalignment. |
| **Breath** | Regulation, recovery, pacing, and the de-escalation of panic. Breath is not an afterthought. In every art, breath is part of the technique itself. |
| **Precision** | Correct line, economy of movement, and deliberate action. Not speed. Not force. The right thing done on the right path. |
| **Threshold Response** | The ability to move from surprise, compromised posture, or sudden contact. The body keeping function when the mind has not yet had time to form a plan. |

| **PART ONE** **IAIDO — THE SWORD PATH** |
| --- |

Iaido is the Japanese martial art of drawing the sword, cutting in a single motion, removing the blood, and returning the sword to the scabbard. It is practiced alone, against an imagined opponent, in a sequence called a kata. There is no sparring. There is no competition. The opponent is always the self.

For the Uncrowned, Iaido develops three things: the ability to organize the entire body around a single intention, the quality of mind that acts decisively without flailing, and the physical practice of zanshin — the sustained, composed awareness that continues after the visible action is complete. It is the art that makes the doctrine physical.

**Equipment Before You Begin**

You do not need a sword to begin. You need correct posture and correct movement. The tool arrives when the body earns it.

| **Stage** | **What You Use** | **Why** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Stage 1 — Foundation** | Empty hand. Mime the grip and draw path with nothing in the hand. This sounds trivial. It is not. Correct form with no tool eliminates the habit of relying on the weight of the object to shape the movement. | *Safety and pattern integrity* |
| **Stage 1–2** | Bokken (wooden sword). A solid hardwood practice sword, typically 40 inches. Available online, $20–$40. This is your primary training tool through all basic stages. | *Safe repetition at full speed over time* |
| **Stage 2–3** | Suburito (heavy practice sword) optionally added for strength and path training. Used slowly, never at speed. | *Builds the line through resistance* |
| **Stage 3+** | Iaito (unsharpened aluminum alloy practice sword). The iaito has the weight, length, and feel of a real katana but no cutting edge. Never transition to an iaito without having first developed correct form. | *Trains with realistic weight and balance* |
| **Stage 4+** | Live blade only under qualified supervision. Do not self-teach live blade work. | *Real consequence requires real guidance* |
| **All stages** | Tatami mat or non-slip flooring. The surface matters for seiza and foot work. | *Safety and correct footwork development* |

## **Seiza — The Kneeling Position**

Seiza is the foundational position of Iaido. Almost all katas begin and end here. Seiza must be learned correctly before any draw is attempted. If your seiza is wrong, everything built on top of it will be wrong.

| **HOW TO ENTER SEIZA — STEP BY STEP** |
| --- |
| Step 1 — Stand upright with your feet together or very slightly apart. The sword (or bokken) is held in the left hand at the scabbard, cutting edge facing upward and away from you, point slightly forward. The sword rides lightly — it is not gripped hard, it is carried. |
| Step 2 — Lower your left knee to the floor first. As you do this, your right knee bends and your body weight shifts onto your right leg while your left knee makes contact. Your left knee lands at roughly a fist-width's distance to the left of where your left foot was standing. |
| Step 3 — Bring your right knee down alongside the left. Both knees are now on the floor, side by side. They should be approximately two fist-widths apart from each other — not pressed together and not splayed wide. |
| Step 4 — Lower your hips back onto your heels. The top surfaces of your feet are flat against the floor, toes pointing straight back. Your buttocks rest on your heels — not in the space between your feet, but on the heels themselves. If this is painful at first, place a folded towel between your heels and your buttocks while you build the required ankle flexibility. |
| Step 5 — Once seated, place the sword across your left thigh, parallel to the floor, cutting edge facing upward. The handle faces left. The length of the sword runs from left to right across your lap. |

| **SEIZA — THE POSITION IN FULL DETAIL** |
| --- |
| THE HIPS: Your hips are settled directly above your heels. The pelvis is neutral — not tipped forward, which would create an exaggerated lower back arch, and not tucked under, which would collapse the lower spine. Think of the sitting bones (the two bony points at the base of your pelvis) as pointing straight down toward the floor, not forward or backward. |
| THE SPINE: From the neutral pelvis, the spine rises vertically. The lumbar (lower back) has its natural gentle inward curve — do not exaggerate it and do not flatten it. The thoracic (mid-back) is gently lifted — not rigid, not collapsed. The cervical (neck) continues the line upward. The crown of the head is the highest point of your body, as if a thread connected it to the ceiling above you and held it lightly. |
| THE CHEST: The sternum (breastbone) is lifted — not thrust out aggressively, but present. Imagine someone placed their index finger on your sternum and asked you to hold it gently rather than collapsing onto their finger. That is the correct position. Your shoulders will naturally settle back and down when the sternum is correctly positioned. |
| THE SHOULDERS: Level with the floor, dropped away from your ears, and neutral — neither pulled back in a military brace nor rounded forward. There should be no tension visible in the tops of your shoulders. If you see or feel ridges of tension across your trapezius, consciously exhale and let the shoulders fall. |
| THE ARMS AND HANDS: Both hands rest on your thighs, palms down. The fingers are together, thumbs resting alongside them. The hands are relaxed. There is no gripping of the thighs. The left hand rests near the scabbard of the sword on your left thigh. |
| THE HEAD: The chin is level with the floor — not tucked down, not raised up. The gaze is forward and slightly downward — approximately 45 degrees below horizontal. This is not a fixed stare. It is a soft, wide gaze that takes in the full field of vision without locking onto any single point. This quality of gaze is called enzan no metsuke — eyes that see the distant mountain. |
| THE BREATH: In seiza, the breath is slow, quiet, and nasal. The belly moves with the breath — the lower abdomen expands on the inhale and gently releases on the exhale. The chest and shoulders do not visibly rise. If they do, the breath is too high and too tight. |

| **Common Fault: **Sitting between the feet with the buttocks on the floor, knees splayed wide. | **Correction: **The buttocks must rest on the heels. Reset by raising the hips until you feel the heel contact, then sit back onto them directly. |
| --- | --- |

| **Common Fault: **Chest collapsed, shoulders rounded forward, lower back slumped. | **Correction: **Place a hand on your sternum and lift it gently. Everything else — shoulders, spine — will organize behind that single correction. |
| --- | --- |

| **Common Fault: **Chin jutting upward or tucked too far down. | **Correction: **Level the chin by finding the point where you can feel equal pressure on the front and back of the base of your skull. That is neutral. |
| --- | --- |

| **Common Fault: **Hands gripping or pressing into the thighs. | **Correction: **Lay the hands onto the thighs like a book placed on a shelf. There is contact but no pressure. |
| --- | --- |

## **Standing Posture — Shizentai**

Shizentai means natural stance. It is the standing equivalent of seiza: the organized, alert, composed default that everything else begins from and returns to. "Natural" here does not mean casual or collapsed. It means the body is organized at its most efficient baseline — no unnecessary tension, no unnecessary slackness.

| **STANDING POSTURE — COMPLETE DESCRIPTION** |
| --- |
| THE FEET: Stand with your feet parallel, approximately shoulder-width apart — or slightly closer. The toes point forward, not turned out. Your weight is distributed evenly across the full surface of both feet: across the heel, the outer edge, and the ball of each foot equally. You should be able to lift your toes slightly without losing balance — this confirms you are not rocking forward onto the balls of the feet. |
| THE KNEES: Soft and unlocked. Not bent as in a squat, not hyperextended backward as in a locked-straight parade-rest position. Simply released — the knee joint at its natural resting angle when the muscles above and below it are relaxed. To find this: stand with legs straight, lock the knees hard backward, feel the tension that creates, then release it. That release is the correct knee position. |
| THE HIPS: The pelvis is neutral. To find this: stand and tip your pelvis forward, exaggerating the lumbar arch (the "duck tail" position). Then tuck it all the way under (the "tucked" or flat-backed position). The correct pelvis is halfway between these two exaggerations — at the midpoint where neither excessive curve nor excessive flatness is present. |
| THE ABDOMEN: The lower abdomen is gently engaged — not sucked in, not pushed out, but present. Think of this as the hara, the center of gravity point approximately two inches below the navel. Awareness of this point, rather than muscular bracing, is the goal. |
| THE SPINE: The natural curves of the spine are maintained. The lumbar (lower back) curves gently inward. The thoracic (mid-back) curves gently outward. The cervical (neck) curves gently inward. These curves are not exaggerated. They are present. The spine is long and decompressed — as if the vertebrae are gently lifted away from each other rather than compressed together. |
| THE CHEST: The sternum is lifted without thrusting. The same cue as in seiza: gently hold the sternum present. The ribcage is neither flared open nor collapsed inward. |
| THE SHOULDERS: Level, dropped, and neutral. Not pinned back, not rolled forward. The shoulder blades are resting flat against the back — not winging out, not squeezed together. The arms hang freely from relaxed shoulder joints. |
| THE ARMS AND HANDS: The left hand rests on the koiguchi (the mouth of the scabbard) or is positioned to receive the scabbard in the ready position. The right hand hangs at the side, relaxed, fingers slightly curled naturally. There is no gripping or fisting. |
| THE HEAD: The head balances directly above the spine. The chin is level. The gaze is forward with the same soft quality as in seiza — enzan no metsuke, the distant mountain. You are looking at the space ahead of you, not at any specific point within it. |
| THE BREATH: Standing nasal breath. The belly leads. The chest follows minimally. The shoulders do not rise. |

| **Common Fault: **Weight rocking forward onto the balls of the feet, creating lean. | **Correction: **Consciously send your weight back into your heels until you feel equal distribution. Then flex the toes slightly off the floor as a test. |
| --- | --- |

| **Common Fault: **Locked knees — the legs rigid and hyperextended backward. | **Correction: **Place both hands on your thighs and gently allow the knees to soften. It is a small release. You will feel the change in the quality of the stance immediately. |
| --- | --- |

| **Common Fault: **Shoulders rising and creating visible tension in the neck. | **Correction: **On an exhale, consciously drop the shoulders an inch further than feels natural. Hold the exhale for two seconds at the bottom. Repeat twice. They will stay lower. |
| --- | --- |

## **The Grip — Tenouchi**

Tenouchi is the art of gripping the sword correctly. Most beginners grip too hard. A tight grip exhausts the forearm, destroys speed, reduces precision, and produces visible jerking in the draw. The correct grip is closer to a firm handshake than a clenching fist.

| **RIGHT HAND GRIP ON THE TSUKA (HANDLE)** |
| --- |
| Place your right hand on the tsuka (handle) with the little finger at the base of the handle, nearest the tsuba (guard). This is the anchor point — the little finger and ring finger provide most of the structural grip. The middle finger is moderate. The index finger and thumb are light — present but not clenching. |
| The thumb and index finger should be able to open slightly without the sword falling. If releasing your thumb and forefinger would cause you to drop the sword, you are gripping too hard with the whole hand. |
| The back of the right wrist is flat or very slightly rounded — not bent upward (which collapses the draw), not forced downward. The forearm, wrist, and back of the hand form a single line when viewed from above. |
| There should be a slight gap — approximately the width of one finger — between the palm and the tsuka. The sword is not pressed into the palm. It rests against the fingers. |
| From the outside, a correct grip looks relaxed. The forearm muscles do not show visible cording or tension. The grip firms at the moment of cut and relaxes immediately after — it is dynamic, not static. |

| **LEFT HAND ON THE SAYA (SCABBARD)** |
| --- |
| The left hand holds the saya (scabbard) near the koiguchi (mouth of the scabbard). The thumb is placed on top of the tsuba (guard) and presses lightly forward — this unlocks the blade from the scabbard, which in a real sword is kept slightly locked by a ridge fit. This action is called koiguchi wo kiru: cutting the koiguchi. |
| The left hand controls the scabbard throughout the draw, pulling it back and slightly down while the right hand draws the blade forward. The saya does not stay still. It moves backward while the blade moves forward. This counter-movement is what creates the clean, efficient draw path. |
| After the draw, the left hand returns to the hip position with the saya held close to the body, or moves to reinforce the two-handed grip depending on the kata. |

## **The Draw — Nukitsuke**

Nukitsuke is the draw-cut — the single motion of drawing the sword from the scabbard and cutting in one continuous arc. It is the central technical skill of Iaido. There is no moment where the draw stops and the cut begins. It is one action.

| **THE DRAW PATH FROM SEIZA — STEP BY STEP** |
| --- |
| PREPARATORY POSITION: In seiza, the sword lies across your left thigh, edge upward. Your left hand is near the koiguchi. Your right hand rests on your right thigh. Your eyes are at the 45-degree soft downward gaze. This is the moment before. |
| STEP 1 — THE INTENTION: Before any physical movement, the mind forms a single, complete intention. In classical Iaido this is the moment of nukite no kokoro — the spirit of the draw. Practically: you are not beginning a physical exercise. You are responding to a situation. The body is about to express a decision that has already been made. This is not theatrical. It is the correct internal sequence. |
| STEP 2 — THUMB PRESS AND GRIP: The left thumb presses lightly on the tsuba, releasing the fit of the blade in the scabbard. Simultaneously, the right hand moves from the right thigh and takes the grip on the tsuka. The right hand grip is from above — the thumb and fingers wrap naturally from the top. The right elbow drops slightly so the arm is in a position to draw horizontally rather than angling upward. |
| STEP 3 — BEGINNING THE DRAW: The right hand begins drawing the blade forward and to the right. This is not a yank. It is a controlled, deliberate slide. As the blade begins to leave the scabbard, the left hand simultaneously begins to pull the saya backward, pressing the koiguchi against the blade to control the exit speed. The two hands move in opposite directions simultaneously — right hand forward, left hand back. This counter-movement is what makes the draw clean. |
| STEP 4 — THE ARC: The draw is not a straight horizontal line. It is a slight arc — the blade traces a curved path as it exits, which corresponds to the arc of a cut. By the time the blade has cleared the scabbard by approximately two-thirds of its length, the right hand is already beginning to extend forward-right in the cutting direction. The arc of the draw becomes the arc of the cut without interruption. |
| STEP 5 — THE CUT COMPLETION: The right arm extends fully, the blade reaches the end of the cut line, and the body has risen from seiza in synchrony with the draw — both knees coming off the floor as the blade exits, the body weight moving forward-right onto a wide stable base. The cut ends with the blade horizontal or slightly downward-diagonal, cutting edge facing the direction of cut, right arm fully extended, left hand holding the saya at the hip. |
| STEP 6 — CHIBURI (BLOOD REMOVAL): After the cut, the sword is shaken to remove blood. In the most common form (o-chiburi): the right arm swings down and to the right in a large arc, the blade cutting downward through a 45-degree angle to the floor on the right side of the body, then arresting cleanly. This is not sloppy. The arrest at the end of chiburi is as precise as the cut. |
| STEP 7 — NOTO (THE RETURN): The sword is returned to the scabbard. The left hand brings the koiguchi forward to meet the blade. The blade rests in the groove made by the left thumb and index finger, which acts as a guide. The blade slides slowly back into the scabbard, cutting edge facing upward throughout. Noto is performed slowly and with absolute control. Speed in noto is how people cut themselves. |

| **Common Fault: **The shoulder hiking up during the draw — the right shoulder rises toward the ear. | **Correction: **Consciously drop the right shoulder before beginning. Draw the blade while imagining the shoulder sinking rather than rising. If it continues to rise, the draw path is too high — lower the target line. |
| --- | --- |

| **Common Fault: **The left hand is static — it does not pull the saya back. | **Correction: **Practice the draw with hands only, no sword, until the left hand counter-movement is automatic. The saya should move back visibly while the right hand draws forward. |
| --- | --- |

| **Common Fault: **The draw is a jerk — a single explosive yank rather than a controlled acceleration. | **Correction: **Practice at one-third speed. The draw should take a full three seconds at slow speed and still feel controlled at that pace before any speed is added. |
| --- | --- |

| **Common Fault: **Rising out of seiza too early or too late — the body movement is not synchronized with the draw. | **Correction: **Practice the rise separately. Begin to rise as the blade crosses the halfway point of the draw. The cut should complete at the same moment the feet find their wide base. |
| --- | --- |

## **The Standing Draw**

The standing draw is simpler in structure but identical in principle to the seiza draw. The difference is in the starting foot position and the absence of the rise.

| **STANDING DRAW — COMPLETE POSITION** |
| --- |
| Begin in shizentai (natural standing posture) as described above. The sword is in the obi (belt) or held in the left hand at carrying position: cutting edge upward, the sword angled slightly forward (approximately 30 degrees from vertical, with the handle forward), held at the scabbard with the left hand near the koiguchi. |
| For a right-side draw, the right foot steps forward and slightly to the right (at approximately the 2 o'clock position from center) as the draw begins. This step is small — no more than one foot-length. Its purpose is to create the stable base for the cut and to bring the right hip forward to clear the sword path. |
| The hips turn slightly to the right as the right foot steps. This hip rotation is part of the draw mechanics — the hips and the draw are not separate. The body is one system. |
| The draw path is the same: right hand draws forward-right, left hand pulls saya back-left, the arc of the draw becomes the arc of the cut. The cut typically ends at horizontal, slightly down-diagonal, or at any height that corresponds to the kata being practiced. |
| After the cut, the feet are in a wide stable base (musubi-dachi or equivalent depending on style). Return to seiza or standing natural posture depending on the kata. |

## **The Five Stages of Iaido**

| **STAGE 1  ****Foundation — Weeks 1–8** |
| --- |
| **▸  **Master seiza — hold correctly for 3 minutes minimum | **▸  **Master shizentai — all postural points check out |
| **▸  **Learn the grip (empty hand first, then bokken) | **▸  **Learn the draw path (slow, with bokken) |
| **▸  **Left hand counter-movement on saya becomes automatic | **▸  **Practice noto until it is clean and controlled |
| **▸  **Log every session — date, duration, primary correction | **▸  **GRADUATION: Hold seiza for 3 min. without collapse. 20 consecutive slow draws with no shoulder hike and consistent path. |

| **STAGE 2  ****Controlled Draw — Weeks 9–20** |
| --- |
| **▸  **Full draw-cut sequence with bokken at controlled speed | **▸  **Extend, cut line, body rise synchronized |
| **▸  **Chiburi practiced for clean arrest at end of arc | **▸  **First short kata learned at very slow tempo |
| **▸  **Film practice from front and side. One correction per session. | **▸  **Begin basic conditioning (see Physical Architecture section) |
| **▸  **Eyes stay soft — no fixing on target | **▸  **GRADUATION: Complete a short kata (Maegiri) 5 times, identical tempo, no loss of balance or shoulder hike. |

| **STAGE 3  ****Sequence and Presence — Months 6–18** |
| --- |
| **▸  **Full seiza kata and standing kata in practice rotation | **▸  **Transition to iaito if posture and draw are genuinely stable |
| **▸  **Zanshin becomes consistent — awareness holds after the cut | **▸  **Multiple kata sequences without rest between |
| **▸  **Earn the hakama and keikogi at this stage — not before | **▸  **Seek one in-person clinic or correction session if possible |

| **STAGE 4  ****Internal Practice — Year 2+** |
| --- |
| **▸  **The practice is now a genuine instrument of self-knowledge | **▸  **Quality of internal state is immediately visible in movement quality |
| **▸  **Practice becomes diagnostic — what does this session reveal? | **▸  **Consider supervised live blade under qualified instruction |
| **▸  **No graduation criterion — this stage does not end | **▸  **Begin teaching another person: the teaching reveals the gaps |
| **▸  **Practice continues to be logged |  |

| **PART TWO** **KYUDO — THE BOW PATH** |
| --- |

Kyudo is the Japanese martial way of archery. It is not sport archery and it is not hunting. It is a contemplative discipline that uses the act of shooting a bow as a mirror for the internal state of the practitioner. The target is present but subordinate — the arrow reveals what no other instrument can: the state of the mind at the moment of release.

For the Uncrowned, Kyudo is the primary developer of the Ni function work — the deliberate cultivation of non-attachment to results. Every INTJ faces the risk of becoming so invested in outcomes that the process itself becomes corrupted. The bow will not tolerate this. When the internal state is grasping, the release will snatch. The form will compensate. The arrow will miss. The practice makes the internal condition immediately and inescapably visible.

**Equipment**

- Beginner yumi (bow): Japanese asymmetric longbow. Entry-level bows are available through online Kyudo suppliers ($80–$200). The handle is positioned roughly one-third up from the bottom — this is not a mistake. The asymmetry is structural.

- Ya (arrows): Kyudo arrows are significantly longer than Western arrows. Measure from the back of the bow at full draw to your draw hand plus a few inches. Carbon or bamboo — carbon is more forgiving and durable for beginners.

- Yugake (glove): Three-finger glove worn on the right (drawing) hand. The thumb catches the string. Beginner yugake until Stage 3, then hand-formed.

- Kake-makura (thumb groove): The string rests in a groove in the yugake thumb. Correct yugake fit matters — a loose yugake produces an inconsistent release.

- Makiwara (practice target): A tightly-wound straw target used at very short range (1 meter) for form development without distance pressure. Essential for Stage 1.

- Safety space: A clear lane a minimum of 28 meters long, with a solid backstop behind the target. Never shoot without a backstop.

## **The Hassetsu — Eight Stages of One Shot**

Every shot in Kyudo passes through exactly eight stages, called hassetsu. These are not steps to hurry through to reach the release. Each stage is complete in itself. The archer who rushes through the early stages to reach hanare (release) is already in the wrong mind for Kyudo.

Below, each of the eight stages is described in complete physical detail. Read each one until you can find the position with your body before reading the next.

| **1 — ASHIBUMI: Foot Placement** |
| --- |
| Stand facing 90 degrees to the right of the target — your body is perpendicular to the target line, the target is directly to your left side. Your feet are together. |
| Step your left foot approximately one shoulder-width to the left, then your right foot the same distance to the right. You are now standing in a wide stance, feet parallel, shoulder-width or slightly wider than shoulder-width apart. |
| Both feet point straight forward (toward the wall you are facing, not toward the target). The feet are flat on the floor with equal weight distribution. |
| The toes of both feet should, if a line were drawn from toe to toe, cross the center of the target when extended. In practice this means the front edge of your left foot is approximately aligned with the target line. |
| The stance feels wide and grounded. You should be able to receive a light push from any direction without stepping. This is the foundation that everything else builds on. |
| Do not move your feet after ashibumi is complete. The feet do not shift during the shot cycle. |

| **2 — DOZUKURI: Body Formation** |
| --- |
| With ashibumi established, organize the whole body upward from the feet. |
| The hips are level and facing directly forward — exactly the same direction as the feet. The hips must not turn toward the target. They remain perpendicular throughout. |
| The pelvis is neutral — the same neutral position as standing posture: neither tipped forward nor tucked under. |
| The spine rises vertically. The chest is open and lifted without forcing or puffing. The sternum is present without being thrust forward. |
| The shoulders are level, dropped, and equidistant from the ears. In Kyudo, this is critical — any difference in shoulder height will create asymmetry in the expansion that follows. |
| The head turns to the left to face the target. This is the only part of the body that faces the target during the entire shot. The chin remains level. The eyes look at the target with the soft gaze of the practitioner who is not trying to hit the target — they are looking at it the way you look at the horizon. |
| The weight is even across both feet. You can feel both feet completely. |
| Spend time here. Breathe into this position. Exhale anything that is not supposed to be here. |

| **3 — YUGAMAE: Readying the Bow** |
| --- |
| The bow is raised from its resting position and the arrow is nocked. In simplified terms: in this stage you are completing the physical preparation of the tool before the action begins. |
| The bow is held in the left hand at the bow grip (nigiri). The correct left hand position: the grip is in the lower heel of the thumb — the fleshy pad at the base of the left thumb — NOT in the palm. This is the most common error of beginners. Gripping the bow in the center of the palm will cause the bow to cant (tilt) at release and will produce inconsistent results. |
| The bow is held with the thumb pointing upward along the left side of the bow. The remaining fingers are relaxed and lightly curved, not gripping. The bow is caught by the thumb pad and the pressure of expansion during the draw — it is not gripped tightly. |
| The right hand (yugake) takes the string with the thumb under the string in the grove of the glove. The arrow nock is seated on the string. The arrow rests on the bow — on the thumb side of the left hand in the traditional Japanese method (rather than on the sight side as in Western archery). |
| The bow and arrows are now at the ready position, held at the right side of the body, bow vertical, both arms relaxed. |

| **4 — UCHIOKOSHI: Raising the Bow** |
| --- |
| Both arms raise the bow directly upward, overhead. This is a bilateral, symmetrical motion — both arms rise together at the same rate. |
| The bow rises until the left arm is extended directly upward, the bow vertical above the head, the right arm bent at approximately 45 degrees with the right elbow at roughly forehead height. |
| Critical: the shoulders do NOT rise with the bow. The shoulders remain down — dropped away from the ears — while the arms rise above them. This requires conscious control. The most common error in uchiokoshi is the shoulders climbing toward the ears, which creates tension in the neck and disrupts the expansion that follows. |
| The back of the left hand faces upward. The bow is now vertical, the string to the right. |
| The chin remains level. The eyes remain on the target. |
| Breathe in during uchiokoshi. |

| **5 — HIKIWAKE: Drawing Apart** |
| --- |
| From the raised position, both arms begin to move simultaneously and in opposite directions — this is the draw. It is not that the right hand pulls the string. Both arms open equally: the left arm (bow arm) moves forward and slightly down toward the target, while the right arm (draw arm) moves backward and slightly down to the right. |
| The word hikiwake means "drawing apart" — the expansion is equal in both directions. If you imagine yourself as a tree whose branches are spreading symmetrically outward, that is the correct quality of the motion. |
| The bow opens as the arms expand. The string comes back toward the right side of the face. The left arm extends toward the target. |
| The shoulder blades draw toward each other as the arms expand — but they are not actively squeezed together. They move toward each other because the arms are spreading, not because someone is pinching the back. |
| The elbows: left elbow is nearly straight but not locked — there should be a very slight curve. The left wrist is straight. The right elbow is bent at approximately 90 degrees or slightly more at the moment the draw reaches full extension, the right elbow is at or slightly above shoulder height, pointing directly to the rear. |
| The draw continues through to kai (the next stage) without stopping. |

| **6 — KAI: The Full Draw** |
| --- |
| Kai is the meeting point — the full draw position, held. There is no fixed duration. A beginner might hold for 3 seconds. An experienced practitioner might hold for 8–10 seconds. What matters is that the hold is active, not passive. |
| At full draw, the string is at the right side of the jaw, touching or near touching the cheek. The right elbow is at shoulder height or slightly above, pointing directly to the right or slightly to the rear. |
| The expansion continues in kai. This is not a position of rest. The bow is drawing the practitioner open. Both arms continue to press outward — the left arm pressing toward the target, the right elbow pressing toward the rear. This is the living quality of kai: active, continuous expansion. |
| The string should be touching or nearly touching the right cheekbone. The arrow is level or near-level, pointing at the target. |
| The breath is held, or in the slow Seitai breathing method, a slow controlled exhale continues through kai and into hanare. |
| The mind at kai must be still. Not focused on hitting. Not analyzing. Present. This is where most practitioners fail — the mind either grasps for the result or becomes distracted. Kai is the pure present, extended. |
| The left hand grip is NOT changing. The bow is still held in the thumb-pad, the fingers still light. A beginner will feel the urge to grip harder here. That urge is wrong. The expansion creates the force. The grip stays consistent. |

| **7 — HANARE: The Release** |
| --- |
| Hanare happens. This is the exact right framing. The archer does not do the release — the release occurs when the expansion has reached the point where the bow's force exceeds the string force and the string rolls off the thumb of the yugake. |
| In correct technique, hanare is not a deliberate opening of the right hand. The right thumb does not consciously let go. The expansion of the right arm has been building pressure against the string, and at the moment of hanare the arm continues backward — the elbow drives straight to the rear, not downward, not upward — and the string leaves the thumb as a result of that continued movement. |
| At the moment of release, the left hand does not grip. The bow rotates in the left hand — this rotation (yugaeri) is correct and intended. The bow rotates forward in the thumb-pad, the string swings to the front of the bow, and the bow itself may swing forward on the thumb. This is the sign of a correct release and a correct left hand position. |
| The right arm continues moving straight to the rear in a smooth arc, ending near shoulder height, pointing to the right. The right hand is open or slightly curved. This continued motion of the right arm after release is not follow-through in the Western archery sense — it is the natural continuation of the expansion that was already in progress. If the right arm stops at the moment of release, the release has been forced. |
| The left arm stays extended toward the target. It does not drop. The bow completes its rotation and the left arm holds the bow extended. |

| **8 — ZANSHIN: Continuing Awareness** |
| --- |
| Zanshin means "remaining mind" or "lingering mind." It is the sustained composure that continues after the visible action is complete. The arrow has left the bow. The shot is over. Zanshin is what happens next — and it is as important as any of the preceding stages. |
| At the moment of hanare, the body has been opened by the expansion. Both arms are extended — left toward the target, right toward the rear. The body is in its widest position. Zanshin is holding this position with complete composure and continued presence for a minimum of two to three seconds after the arrow has reached the target. |
| The archer does not immediately drop the arms, turn to look at the target, or relax the posture. The posture is maintained. The eyes continue looking at the target with the same soft quality they had in kai. |
| Breathing resumes naturally during zanshin. The exhale that was begun in the earlier stages completes here. |
| Zanshin is where the quality of the shot — and the quality of the mind during the shot — is most honestly revealed. A practitioner who executed the form correctly but grabbed for the result will show it here: in a slight collapse, a premature drop of the arms, a shift of the eyes before the appropriate pause. The arrow cannot be called back, but zanshin reveals whether the mind was clean. |
| Only after zanshin has completed fully does the practitioner lower the arms, bring the bow back to the carrying position, and prepare for the next arrow or end the session. |

| **Common Fault: **Shoulders rising during uchiokoshi and remaining high through the shot. | **Correction: **Before any upward arm movement, consciously exhale and drop the shoulders down. Begin uchiokoshi from that dropped-shoulder starting position. If they rise with the arms, stop, reset, and begin again. This correction cannot be skipped. |
| --- | --- |

| **Common Fault: **Right hand opening deliberately at hanare — forcing the release. | **Correction: **Practice with a resistance band instead of a bow. Draw the band back with the right elbow continuing to drive to the rear. Do not open the hand. Let the band roll off the thumb from the continued elbow movement. When this feels natural with the band, return to the bow. |
| --- | --- |

| **Common Fault: **Left hand gripping at full draw, preventing yugaeri. | **Correction: **At full draw, consciously think "thumb pad only" and allow the remaining fingers to lighten. The bow rotation after release is the confirmation. If the bow does not rotate, the left hand is gripping too hard. |
| --- | --- |

**Band Training — Building Kyudo Mechanics Without a Bow**

A resistance band tied to a door handle or post at shoulder height can train the Kyudo draw expansion safely and repeatably. Use a medium-resistance band. This is the recommended Stage 1 approach before using a real bow.

- Position yourself in ashibumi stance perpendicular to the anchor point.

- Take the band with the right hand (yugake position — thumb under the band).

- Raise both arms into uchiokoshi, then begin hikiwake: left arm extends toward the anchor, right arm draws back from it.

- At full draw position (kai), hold for 5 seconds actively expanding. The shoulder blades draw toward each other. The left arm presses toward the anchor. The right elbow drives to the rear.

- Release by continuing the right elbow's backward movement. Do not open the fingers.

- Hold zanshin for 3 seconds. Assess: were the shoulders level? Was the expansion equal? Did the right arm continue after release?

| **PART THREE** **SYSTEMA — THE THRESHOLD ART** |
| --- |

Systema is a Russian martial art developed originally for military and special operations use. Unlike the structured forms of Iaido and Kyudo, Systema has no fixed stances, no katas, no belt ranks, and no competitions. It is a principles-based system that addresses a specific and practical problem: what happens to the body and mind when violence begins before you are ready for it.

The INTJ threat profile is specific: not aggressive confrontation (the Uncrowned typically avoids those), but sudden close-distance surprise — grab, shove, collision — at conversational distance from a nonchalant or distracted posture. Systema exists precisely for this. It trains breathing instead of bracing, movement instead of freezing, and exit-seeking instead of dominance-seeking.

*Systema does not replace Iaido or Kyudo. It completes them. Iaido teaches what to do when you are ready. Systema teaches what to do when you are not.*

## **The Five Principles**

| **PRINCIPLE 1 — BREATHE FIRST** |
| --- |
| The body's most dangerous response to sudden force is breath-holding. Held breath creates rigidity. Rigid muscles absorb impact badly and move poorly. Every Systema drill begins with explicit attention to the breath: before you react physically, you breathe. This is not metaphysical instruction. It is neurological. The exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system and damps the panic-rigidity response. |
| In practice: when something unexpected happens — a touch, a shout, a stumble — the first conscious act is an exhale, not a movement. The movement follows the exhale. This is trained until it becomes automatic, because under genuine surprise, untrained automatic responses take over. You are training the automatic response. |

| **PRINCIPLE 2 — STAY LOOSE** |
| --- |
| Softness is not weakness. In Systema, the body is kept loose — joints mobile, muscles not braced, jaw unclenched, shoulders unlocked — because a loose body moves through force rather than receiving it. A braced, rigid body absorbs all the energy of a push, a hit, or a throw. A loose body redirects much of that energy. |
| The loose body is not a floppy body. Structure is still present — the spine is still organized, the core still provides center. The looseness lives in the peripheral joints and muscle groups: shoulders, elbows, wrists, hips, knees, ankles. These move freely while the spine provides the organizing line. |
| In practice: before each drill, scan from the feet upward. Jaw unclenched. Shoulders dropped. Elbows not locked. Hands open or softly curved. Knees not rigid. This scan takes three seconds. It is not optional. |

| **PRINCIPLE 3 — MOVE FROM WHERE YOU ARE** |
| --- |
| You will rarely be in an ideal position when something happens. The natural untrained response is to try to establish the ideal position first, then respond. This creates a fatal delay. Systema teaches moving from exactly where you are — seated, one hand full, mid-conversation, turned the wrong way. |
| This principle means there are no fixed defensive stances in Systema. If you are sitting and a grab begins, you respond from sitting, not after first standing. If you are mid-step and contact happens, you respond from the mid-step position, not after first establishing a stable base. |
| In practice: all Systema drills begin from non-readiness postures — sitting in a chair, standing casually with weight on one foot, walking, carrying something in one hand. The drill begins before the practitioner is ready. |

| **PRINCIPLE 4 — ESCAPE AND REPOSITION** |
| --- |
| The goal of Systema for the Uncrowned is not victory in combat. It is functional exit from a dangerous situation. The primary tactical objective is always: create enough space and time to either escape the situation entirely or reach a state where you can act from composure rather than panic. |
| This means stepping off the line of force is always preferable to meeting force head-on. Stepping to the side, backward, or at an angle — getting your body out of where the force was aimed — is the first movement in most Systema responses. |
| In practice: in every drill, the first movement is always off-line. Step to the side of the grab. Step back and to the angle of the push. Create distance first. Everything else is secondary. |

| **PRINCIPLE 5 — KEEP THE BODY AVAILABLE** |
| --- |
| The body must remain capable of function throughout a threshold encounter. This means protecting the structural components that allow movement: the spine is never deliberately exposed, the head is protected from impact through movement rather than blocking, the joints are kept mobile, and the breath is kept moving throughout. |
| An unavailable body is one that has committed fully to a single action and cannot recover from it. Systema practitioners develop the habit of never fully committing the weight to an action without having the next movement already possible. There is always a way out. |
| In practice: this is trained through the recovery drills — falling and returning to base without pause, exiting a grab by moving the whole body rather than wrestling the grabbed limb, and standing up from the floor under pressure using breath and sequential movement rather than muscular effort. |

## **Self-Teaching Drills**

These can be practiced alone. They require no partner, no equipment, and minimal space. They are the foundation of Systema competence.

**Drill 1 — Breath Under Tension**

Stand in natural posture. Breathe slowly in through the nose for four counts. Hold for two counts. Exhale through the mouth for six counts. While breathing, deliberately tense a muscle group — the fists, the shoulders, the whole body — and then release on the exhale. Repeat with the tension arriving during the hold and releasing during the exhale. This trains the exhale as a release mechanism, so the body begins to associate exhale with reduced tension.

Progress: walk slowly while doing the same breath pattern. Then walk and change direction suddenly mid-breath. The breath should not be interrupted by the change of direction. The breath continues; the body moves around it.

**Drill 2 — Non-Readiness Movement**

Begin from a casual standing position — weight slightly on one foot, one hand in a pocket, looking away from the room. On a self-given cue (a single clap, a word spoken aloud), move to a wide, stable two-foot base with the spine organized and both hands free. Time from cue to stable base: under 1.5 seconds is the initial goal. The movement should be one continuous motion, not a series of steps.

Progress: begin seated in a chair. On cue, stand, step off the original line, and establish base. Begin with hands full — holding a book, a cup. The task is to set it down, create distance, and find base in a fluid sequence.

**Drill 3 — Wrist Grab Escape**

With a self-applied grip on your own right wrist with the left hand (or practiced with a partner), practice escaping the grab without arm wrestling. The key insight: a grab can only hold if the grabbed limb moves against the grab, or if the grabbed limb stays still while the grabber maintains position. Either direction of arm movement can be neutralized by moving the whole body instead.

- ESCAPE A — The Rotation: Rotate the entire forearm in the direction the fingers of the grabbing hand are pointing. If the grab is thumb-up on your wrist, rotate your arm so the thumb side rotates outward (supination). The grab is forced to either release or break its own grip.

- ESCAPE B — Step into the grab: Step directly toward the grabbing hand (toward the person grabbing you). This creates slack in the grab — the grabber was expecting you to pull away and their grip was calibrated for that. Your step forward creates distance in the wrong direction for the grab and allows your arm to exit.

- ESCAPE C — The whole-body exit: Rather than moving the grabbed arm, let the arm stay where it is and move the rest of the body away from the grab — step to the side, rotate the hips, turn the body. The arm follows the body rather than fighting the grab.

Practice all three until they are available automatically, not recalled from thought.

**Drill 4 — Ground Recovery**

Begin lying on the floor on your back. Without using your hands to push off the floor: roll to one side, bring one knee under the body, come to a kneeling position, then stand. The entire sequence is breath-led — begin with an exhale, and each stage transition happens on an exhale. The breath is not held at any point.

Progress: begin from supine and come to standing in 3 seconds or under. Then: do the same with hands occupied (held to the sides, or wrapped in front). Then: do it in a restricted space.

**Drill 5 — The Systema Walk**

Walk normally. As you walk, maintain active awareness of: breath (nasal, continuous), shoulders (down and open), jaw (unclenched), hands (unclenched and available), feet (full contact with floor, not just ball of foot). This is the full-body softness scan in motion. At a random self-chosen interval, change direction. Then stop suddenly. Then step sideways. The movement should happen without any of the five monitored states collapsing.

| **PART FOUR** **THROWING DAGGERS — THE ALTERNATIVE DISCIPLINE** |
| --- |

Throwing daggers are the alternative to Kyudo: they develop precision, kinesthetic calibration, and present-moment feedback in a compact, portable format. When travel prevents bow practice, when the session needs to be under fifteen minutes, or when the practitioner needs the immediate feedback that kinesthetic precision provides, throwing is the correct alternative.

The dagger does not develop the same depth of non-attachment and internal stillness that Kyudo structures. That is not a flaw — it is the correct tool for a different function. Know what it develops, use it for that purpose, and do not try to make it into something it is not.

## **Equipment and Safety**

- PURPOSE-MADE THROWING KNIVES ONLY. Never use kitchen knives, decorative knives, folding knives, or knives with fragile handles. Purpose-made throwing knives are designed to take the impact of striking a target. They are single-piece or fully-assembled steel construction with no moving parts. Recommended weight: 200–300 grams per knife. Starting set of 3 identical knives.

- TARGET: End-grain wood or a dedicated throwing target (compressed straw, fiber board, or commercial throwing target board). The target must absorb the blade without destroying it. Soft woods like pine work. Hardwoods like oak will damage blades. Minimum target size: 24 inches diameter or square.

- SAFETY LANE: A completely clear lane behind and around the target. No foot traffic. No objects that could cause a ricochet. A solid backstop (solid wood, earth bank, or commercial target backing) must exist behind the target.

- RETRIEVAL PROTOCOL: Always approach the target from the side, never from the lane. Wait until all throwing is complete before retrieving. Handle retrieved blades by the handle only.

## **The Throwing Stance**

Throwing stance is simpler than Iaido or Kyudo stance but still matters. Inconsistent stance is the most common cause of inconsistent throws that are otherwise mechanically correct.

| **HAMMER GRIP THROWING STANCE — COMPLETE DESCRIPTION** |
| --- |
| THE FEET: Stand facing the target directly. Right foot forward (for right-hand throwers), left foot back. The feet are shoulder-width apart in the forward-back direction — not closer, not wider. The right toes point toward the target. The left foot is at a natural angle (roughly 30–45 degrees outward from the target line). |
| THE WEIGHT: Approximately 60% of your weight is on your right (front) foot at the start of the throw. As the throwing arm comes forward, the weight shifts forward until at the moment of release approximately 80% of the weight is on the front foot. |
| THE HIPS: At the start of the throw, the hips are open — facing the target squarely. This is not a rotational throw. The power does not come from hip rotation (as in a baseball pitch). It comes from the arm swing and the forward weight transfer. The hips stay approximately square to the target throughout. |
| THE NON-THROWING ARM: The left arm helps with balance and timing. It hangs naturally or extends slightly to the left for balance as the right arm swings. It does not do anything specific — it counterbalances. |
| THE SHOULDERS: Relaxed, level, and square to the target at the start. The right shoulder will naturally come forward as the throwing arm swings — this is correct. It should not be forced. It should not be suppressed. |

| **THE HAMMER GRIP — STAGE 1 GRIP** |
| --- |
| Pick up the knife at the balance point of the handle — approximately in the middle of the handle, not at the very end. The handle runs through the palm from the base of the index finger down through the heel of the palm to the base of the pinky. |
| Close all four fingers around the handle, with the thumb either lying along the top of the handle (parallel to the blade spine) or wrapped alongside the fingers. The grip is firm but not white-knuckle. The same "firm handshake" quality as the Iaido tenouchi applies here. |
| The blade faces forward — the point aims in the direction you are throwing. The cutting edge (if there is one) faces down or to the side depending on the knife. For purely rotational throwing knives, edge direction is less important than consistent orientation relative to the handle. |
| The wrist is neutral — not flexed upward (snap throw) and not forced downward. The wrist stays fixed in a neutral position throughout the throw. This is the single most important mechanical point for beginners. |

| **THE THROW — ARM ARC AND RELEASE** |
| --- |
| STEP 1: Begin with the throwing arm raised, the knife held at roughly ear height or slightly above, the elbow bent at approximately 90 degrees. The knife is behind the head to the right, point toward the ceiling. This is the starting position. |
| STEP 2: The throwing arm swings forward in a vertical arc — straight forward and down, not sideways. Think of the arm as the hand of a clock swinging from 12 o'clock to 6 o'clock. The elbow leads slightly, the forearm follows. |
| STEP 3: Release occurs when the arm has swung to approximately the 2–3 o'clock position (between straight forward and slightly downward). The exact release point depends on the distance to the target — releasing earlier (higher) sends the blade nose-down; releasing later (lower) sends it nose-up. At 3 meters with a single-rotation technique, the release point is approximately when the forearm points straight at the target. |
| STEP 4: The wrist does NOT snap. This is the most common beginner error. The wrist stays neutral throughout the swing and at the moment of release. The knife leaves the hand because the fingers open — not because the wrist flicks. A wrist snap adds unpredictable rotation that is very difficult to calibrate. |
| STEP 5: The fingers open as the arm passes through the release point. The knife leaves from between the fingers and thumb. The hand continues forward a short distance naturally — there is no violent stopping of the arm. |
| STEP 6: The body continues forward slightly as the weight shifts to the front foot. The chest drops slightly forward. The throwing arm finishes pointing toward the target or slightly down from the target. |

| **Common Fault: **The knife hits handle-first (under-rotation). The knife rotated less than expected. | **Correction: **Move slightly closer to the target, OR release the throw fractionally earlier (higher in the arc). One of these two adjustments will add rotation. |
| --- | --- |

| **Common Fault: **The knife hits blade-spine-first (over-rotation). It passed through a full rotation and went too far. | **Correction: **Move slightly farther from the target, OR release slightly later (lower in the arc). |
| --- | --- |

| **Common Fault: **Throws are inconsistent — sticking sometimes but not repeatably. | **Correction: **The wrist is snapping or the grip is changing between throws. Lock the wrist neutral. Standardize the grip placement before every throw. Same grip, same release point, same distance. |
| --- | --- |

| **Common Fault: **The body is lurching or stepping forward during the throw. | **Correction: **The stance is too narrow in the forward-back direction. Widen the stance. Or, the weight transfer is too aggressive. Focus on smooth transfer rather than a lunge. |
| --- | --- |

## **The Four Stages of Throwing**

| **STAGE 1  ****Safety and Distance — Weeks 1–4** |
| --- |
| **▸  **Establish target lane with clear backstop | **▸  **Learn hammer grip: consistent hand placement before every throw |
| **▸  **Single fixed distance: 3 meters exactly (measure it) | **▸  **Single-rotation technique only |
| **▸  **Neutral wrist — no snapping | **▸  **Log every session: how many thrown, how many stuck, specific fault observed |
| **▸  **GRADUATION: 30 consecutive safe releases, all throws on the board, consistent blade orientation at release. |  |

| **STAGE 2  ****Repeatability — Weeks 5–10** |
| --- |
| **▸  **Consistent results at 3m before any other distance is introduced | **▸  **Sets of 5 throws from one distance — log stick rate |
| **▸  **Introduce 5-meter distance only when 3m is reliable | **▸  **Learn blade grip at 3m (second rotation) |
| **▸  **Film from the side to check wrist and arm arc consistency | **▸  **GRADUATION: 60% clean sticks across three separate sessions at 3m. |

| **STAGE 3  ****Precision and Adjustment** |
| --- |
| **▸  **Three working distance-grip combinations | **▸  **Can explain why a miss occurred (not just that it missed) |
| **▸  **Introduce multiple targets in a session | **▸  **Grouping work: three throws within 6-inch circle at 3m |
| **▸  **No-spin technique introduced if desired | **▸  **GRADUATION: Able to explain correction for any miss. Three-throw group within 6 inches at 3m. |

| **STAGE 4  ****Internal Practice — Beyond Stage 3** |
| --- |
| **▸  **Emotional state does not accelerate the hand after a miss | **▸  **Same calm release whether the last throw stuck or not |
| **▸  **Practice used as a present-moment diagnostic | **▸  **When the state is clean, the knife lands. Use this as information. |
| **▸  **Practice continues to be logged |  |

| **PART FIVE** **PHYSICAL ARCHITECTURE** |
| --- |

The Physical Architecture is the conditioning base that makes the arts possible without injury and sustainable without burnout. It is not separate from the practice. It serves the practice. Every element of the conditioning program exists because it directly supports one or more of the four pillars.

## **Assessment Before Beginning**

Before building a training structure, you need an honest inventory of the current physical state. These are the questions to answer before Week 1:

- Hip mobility: can you sit in seiza for two minutes without significant pain or numbness? If not, ankle and hip mobility work precedes sword practice.

- Shoulder mobility: can you raise both arms directly overhead without pain, both arms reaching vertical? If not, shoulder mobility work precedes Kyudo expansion drills.

- Grip endurance: can you hold a firm grip for 30 seconds without forearm fatigue becoming the limiting factor in a drill? If not, grip and forearm conditioning is a week-one priority.

- Nasal breathing: can you walk briskly for 10 minutes while breathing exclusively through the nose? If not, nasal breathing conditioning begins immediately as a daily practice, separate from all other training.

- Lower back: any history of lower back issues that are aggravated by kneeling, hinging, or forward bending? Note these before designing the strength protocol.

## **The Weekly Training Structure**

Five training days and two recovery days per week. This is not negotiable in the early months. The arts require neurological recovery as much as physical recovery — the patterns being built live in the nervous system, and a fatigued nervous system does not encode new patterns cleanly.

| **Day** | **Focus** | **Detail** | **Exit Condition** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Day 1** | **Iaido + Lower Body** | Draw path, seiza, standing posture. Lower-body strength: squat, hip hinge, step. | *End when draw line degrades* |
| **Day 2** | **Kyudo/Band + Zone 2** | Expansion drills, hassetsu walkthrough, band release work. 30-min Zone 2 walk. | *End with calm breath* |
| **Day 3** | **Systema Threshold** | Non-readiness drills, grab escapes, ground recovery, breath under tension. | *End with clean exits* |
| **Day 4** | **Iaido Sequence + Core** | Kata sequences, transitions. Core work: anti-rotation, carries, planks. | *End with 5 matched reps* |
| **Day 5** | **Kyudo/Dagger + Pulls** | Full shot sequence or throwing session. Rows, pull-ups, face pulls. | *End before shrug appears* |
| **Day 6** | **Review or Recovery** | Film review, light form work, mobility, Zone 2 walk, or complete rest. | *End with log entry* |
| **Day 7** | **Off** | No training. Walk if desired. Read. Let the week process. | *—* |

## **The Foundation Movements**

These six movements form the strength base. They are not a bodybuilding program. Each movement was selected because it directly addresses the physical demands of at least one of the arts.

| **Movement** | **Why It Belongs Here** | **Starting Volume** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Goblet Squat or Box Squat** | Develops seiza entry/exit strength, leg endurance for extended practice, and hip mobility under load. | *3 sets × 8 reps, moderate weight* |
| **Romanian Deadlift (Hip Hinge)** | Develops posterior chain for the standing draw, back endurance for extended seiza, and safe bending mechanics. | *3 × 8, light to moderate weight* |
| **Rear-Foot Elevated Split Squat** | Single-leg strength and hip flexibility. The standing draw is a split-weight position. This trains it. | *3 × 8 each side, bodyweight first* |
| **Dumbbell Row or Band Row** | Develops the scapular retraction and shoulder strength required for Kyudo expansion at draw weight. | *3 × 10–12 per side* |
| **Face Pull or Band Pull-Apart** | Trains the external rotators and rear deltoids that stabilize the draw position and prevent shoulder impingement. | *3 × 15, light resistance* |
| **Farmer****'****s Carry** | Grip endurance, posture under load, walking stability, and mental resolve. All four pillars in one movement. | *3 × 40 meters, moderate weight* |

**Nasal Breathing — The Daily Practice**

Nasal breathing is not a preference — it is a physical discipline that underpins every art in this practice. The nose warms, humidifies, and filters air, and nasal breathing produces nitric oxide which improves oxygen delivery. More importantly for the practitioner: nasal breathing is anatomically connected to calmer, more regulated nervous system states than mouth breathing. A practitioner who mouth-breathes in practice is training the panic response into the arts they are trying to build composure in.

- Daily target: all breathing during Zone 2 walks and non-strenuous activity is nasal.

- During technique practice: all breathing is nasal throughout.

- Under moderate exertion: transition to nasal breathing and slow the pace to maintain it, rather than opening the mouth.

- Recovery standard: after a demanding bout, measure the time to return to comfortable nasal breath. Early in training this may be 2–3 minutes. At maturity, 30–45 seconds is the standard.

| **PART SIX** **LOGGING, TESTING, AND PROGRESSION** |
| --- |

The log is not optional. It is the evidence that practice is happening and the only honest record of progression. Memory inflates success and forgets failure. The log corrects for this.

| **Field** | **What to Record** | **Required** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Date / Duration** | Date, start time, duration of session in minutes | *Yes* |
| **Sleep (prior night)** | Hours and quality. Poor sleep explains coordination and patience issues. | *Yes* |
| **Emotional state** | One or two words. Agitated, flat, present, distracted, focused. | *Yes* |
| **Primary arts worked** | Which arts. Which stage. | *Yes* |
| **Technical focus** | The one specific correction that was the session's target. | *Yes* |
| **Main fault observed** | What actually went wrong — named specifically, not generally. | *Yes* |
| **Best repetition** | What it felt like. This anchors the success pattern for next time. | *Yes* |
| **Breath quality** | Was nasal breathing maintained throughout? If not, when did it break? | *Yes* |
| **Recovery note** | How long to return to calm nasal breath after the hardest bout. | *Optional* |
| **Next session****'****s first correction** | Decided now, while the session is fresh. | *Yes* |

**Weekly Review — Every Seventh Session**

Once per week, write a short paragraph answering three questions:

- What became more reliable this week?

- What still breaks down first under pressure or fatigue?

- What simplified one-word cue seems to help the most for the primary fault?

Weekly reflection turns raw data into actual learning. Without this step, the log is just records.

**The 90-Day Test**

At day 90 from beginning the practice, the practitioner must be able to:

- Show a complete, unbroken logbook from Day 1.

- Demonstrate the warm-up sequence from memory without reference.

- Name the current stage in each discipline being practiced and state the graduation criteria for the next stage.

- Identify the three most persistent technical errors with their specific corrections — not "my seiza needs work" but "my right shoulder rises on the draw because my right elbow position is too high entering the draw, and the correction is to drop the elbow before beginning."

If any of these cannot be demonstrated, return to Stage 1 of the relevant art. Not as punishment. As the honest starting point.

**The 12-Month Roadmap**

- Months 1–3: Shape, safety, and honest logging. Learn how to set posture, breathe on purpose, and perform low-complexity repetitions. Leave each session with one clear correction. Impatience is the main enemy.

- Months 4–6: Repeatability. Film regularly. The first repetition and the fifth must come from the same nervous system. Strength and conditioning should now be consistent enough that technical sessions feel physically supported.

- Months 7–9: Pressure without panic. Add longer uninterrupted sequences, mild threshold drills, slightly longer expansion holds, and transitions between arts. The goal is not drama — it is preserving line when the task becomes less convenient.

- Months 10–12: Expression through simplicity. The form is simpler, not more elaborate. The corrections are subtler. The internal quality of each session is more consistently present than it was in the first months.
